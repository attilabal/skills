# Operating Modes

Use this reference when executing a selected mode.

## 1. Single-Pass

Use for low-risk work where speed and clarity matter more than independent review.

Examples:

- short internal email draft;
- quick meeting note;
- simple status wording;
- lightweight document rewrite;
- first-pass opinion clearly framed as informal.

Still apply basic source discipline. Low risk does not mean permission to invent facts.

## 2. Two-Pass

Use when the work deserves separation between creation and review.

### Self-Review Two-Pass

Use when the task is moderately important but internal or low-to-medium risk.

Pattern:

1. Draft or solve.
2. Switch to reviewer posture.
3. Check source support, tone, overclaims, missing risks, contradictions, and usability.
4. Revise once.

### Separate Reviewer Two-Pass

Use when the material is client-facing, executive-facing, decision-facing, sensitive, or reputationally exposed.

Pattern:

1. Implementer creates the first output.
2. Reviewer receives the output and relevant inputs.
3. Reviewer looks only for defects: unsupported claims, overconfidence, missing caveats, tone problems, source gaps, edge cases, and decision risk.
4. Finalizer incorporates valid feedback and rejects noise.

Reviewer feedback should be specific and actionable. Avoid generic "be careful" warnings.

## 3. Cost-Aware Delegation

Use when a high-reasoning lead agent can safely delegate bounded processing to a cheaper or narrower worker.

Good worker tasks:

- extract facts, dates, parties, obligations, risks, or open questions from documents;
- list project statuses, dependencies, blockers, and unresolved issues;
- extract customer requirements, stakeholder mentions, RFP requirements, or competitor references;
- classify a source subset;
- find gaps, inconsistencies, duplicates, or known patterns.

Bad worker tasks:

- decide the final business position;
- independently write sensitive client communication;
- make final legal, reputational, financial, or strategic risk judgments;
- resolve vague tasks with heavy implicit context;
- produce conclusions the lead agent will not verify.

### Delegation Card

Before delegation, define:

- **Subtask:** what exactly should be done?
- **Why delegable:** why is this safe to delegate?
- **Worker capability:** what level or kind of worker is enough?
- **Input:** what sources are provided?
- **Forbidden decisions:** what must the worker not decide?
- **Return format:** what shape should the worker output have?
- **Verification level:** Level 0, 1, 2, or 3.
- **Return-to-lead conditions:** when should the worker stop or flag uncertainty?

### Verification Ladder

- **Level 0 - lead spot check:** Use for low-risk extraction or classification.
- **Level 1 - one reviewer:** Use when worker output affects final claims or external/executive communication.
- **Level 2 - two independent verifiers:** Use when errors could create reputational, legal, financial, sales, or project risk.
- **Level 3 - fixer loop:** Use when verifiers find errors, contradictions, or weak inference in a critical or disputed subtask.

After verification, the lead decides accept, revise, or reject.

## 4. Small Role Team

Use when the task needs perspective diversity.

Choose only roles that the task justifies. Examples:

- PMO / project lead;
- sales / account lead;
- legal or compliance reviewer;
- finance / commercial reviewer;
- customer communication reviewer;
- quality reviewer.

Pattern:

1. Name each role and its mandate.
2. Keep each role narrow.
3. Ask each role for findings, not full parallel essays.
4. Synthesize into one coherent answer.
5. Mark unresolved disagreements.

Do not create a role team just to make the output look sophisticated.

## 5. Larger Workflow

Use when work is genuinely large and risky.

Signals:

- many documents, emails, CRM records, or project artifacts;
- multiple separable work packages;
- executive, client, contract, RFP, or decision use;
- independent verification matters;
- source gaps and contradictions are likely;
- final synthesis must reconcile several streams.

Pattern:

1. Define work packages.
2. Decide which packages need workers.
3. Assign verification levels.
4. Run workers/reviewers in bounded roles.
5. Resolve disagreements and weak claims.
6. Produce a unified final answer.
7. Include constraints and open questions at the right visibility level.
